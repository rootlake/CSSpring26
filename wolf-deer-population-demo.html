Wolves and elk in Yellowstone National Park

Elk (Cervus canadensis) are cervids—the same family as deer, moose, and caribou—and are the main large ungulate monitored on Yellowstone’s northern range alongside gray wolves. Wolves are year-end totals inside the park (NPS Wolf Project). Elk benchmarks are winter aerial-style counts of the Northern Yellowstone elk herd; values between survey years are linearly interpolated for plotting only.

Dual vertical scales: left axis = wolves; right axis = elk. Correlation is not causation—hunting, bears, drought, and winter severity also moved elk numbers.

Sources

  • Wolves (1995–2024): Yellowstone National Park Wolf Project year-end totals (e.g. annual reports linked from NPS Yellowstone wolf reports); same figures are tabulated in the Wikipedia article Yellowstone wolves for convenience.
  • Elk benchmarks: 1995 and 2004 northern herd counts (~16,791 and ~8,335) as summarized in ecological assessments of the northern range (widely cited alongside wolf recovery); 2008 winter survey (~6,279) reported in press summaries of the cooperative aerial count; ~6,037 (2010) and ~3,915 (2013) from northern herd reporting in park/cooperator news summaries; March 2024 classification count 5,597 in NPS 2024 Wolf, Cougar, & Elk Report (PDF). Interpolated years are illustrative connectors only.
